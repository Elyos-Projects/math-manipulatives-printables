Concrete manipulatives — counters, number lines, fraction tiles, base-ten blocks — are one of the best-evidenced tools in early and elementary mathematics: they let a learner *see and touch* the quantity behind a symbol before the symbol means anything. Commercial sets cost real money (a class set of fraction tiles or base-ten blocks runs tens to hundreds of dollars), so the children who would benefit most — under-resourced classrooms, homeschooling and unschooling families, refugee and crisis-schooling settings, tutoring and after-school programs — are exactly the ones least likely to have them. A free, **print-it-yourself** manipulative that works on an ordinary black-and-white office printer closes that gap with paper and scissors.

**Definition of shipped:** secured channel, verified dimensionally + physically + for accessibility, and educator-reviewed**, so a real learner can obtain it and print a *correct, usable* manipulative. Generated-but-unverified, or verified-but-undistributed, ≠ shipped.
